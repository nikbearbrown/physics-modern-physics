# Chapter 11 — The Universe's First Three Minutes: Evidence for the Beginning
*How three independent observations — galaxies flying apart, ancient microwave light, and the ratio of hydrogen to helium — converge on a single story.*

In 1964, Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson at Bell Laboratories in New Jersey were trying to build a sensitive radio antenna and kept finding a persistent hiss in their receiver. It was not interference from New York City. It was not thermal noise from the equipment. It came uniformly from all directions in the sky and did not go away at any time of day or any season of the year. They cleaned pigeon droppings from the antenna horn. The hiss remained.

What they had discovered — without intending to — was the oldest light in the universe. It had been traveling unimpeded for 13.8 billion years. It had started as the glow of a plasma hotter than any star, and the expansion of the universe had stretched it to microwave wavelengths and cooled it to 2.73 degrees above absolute zero. It was everywhere, arriving from every direction, because it had originated everywhere simultaneously, in an epoch when the entire observable universe was a single hot plasma.

This is the cosmic microwave background. But it is only one of three completely independent observations that all point to the same story. Each is worth understanding on its own terms, because together they are almost impossible to dismiss.

---

## Running the Film Backward

Start with the simplest observation and the most dramatic implication.

Edwin Hubble published a result in 1929 that changed cosmology permanently: distant galaxies are receding, and the farther away a galaxy is, the faster it moves away. The relationship is linear:

$$v = H_0 \times d$$

where $v$ is the recession velocity, $d$ is the distance, and $H_0$ is the Hubble constant — currently measured at about 70 kilometers per second per megaparsec. A galaxy one megaparsec away (roughly 3.26 million light-years) recedes at 70 km/s. A galaxy ten megaparsecs away recedes at 700 km/s.

The immediate implication is almost too simple. If everything is currently moving away from everything else, then running the film backward in time brings everything together. At some moment in the past — not infinitely remote, but a finite time ago — all the matter and energy we observe was compressed into a single region. The time since that moment is roughly $1/H_0$, which works out to about 14 billion years. The more careful calculation, accounting for the fact that expansion has not been constant (gravity slowed it early on; something is accelerating it now), gives 13.8 billion years.

![Hubble's 1929 data. The farther the galaxy, the faster it recedes. Run this backward and everything was once together.](images/11-the-big-bang-fig-01.png)
*Figure 11.1 — Hubble's law *

This is not a strange thing to find. It is the ordinary consequence of the ordinary observation that things are moving apart.

Now, the expanding universe by itself does not tell you what the universe was like at the beginning — only that it was compressed. For that, you need to ask what physics was doing during the compression. And physics, during the compression, was doing nuclear reactions.

---

## What Happens When You Compress Everything

Here is the key insight of Big Bang nucleosynthesis, worked out by George Gamow and Ralph Alpher in 1948: in the first few minutes after the Big Bang, the universe was hot enough for nuclear fusion. After those first few minutes, it was too cool and too dilute. There was a narrow window — about three minutes wide — during which the light elements could form. What came out of those three minutes is calculable from known nuclear physics. And what came out matches what we observe in the oldest material in the universe.

Trace the sequence.

At one second after the Big Bang, the temperature was about 10 billion Kelvin. Free neutrons and protons existed in thermal equilibrium — constantly converting into each other through reactions involving electrons, positrons, and neutrinos. As the temperature dropped, these conversion reactions became too slow to keep pace. The neutron-to-proton ratio froze at roughly one neutron for every six protons. This ratio matters because it determines everything that follows.

At about a tenth of a second later, the temperature dropped below the threshold at which deuterium — one proton and one neutron — can survive without being immediately photodissociated. Deuterium formed and was immediately broken apart. But as the universe continued to cool, deuterium began surviving long enough to react further. Deuterium combined with neutrons to form tritium, or with protons to form helium-3. Helium-3 combined with neutrons to form helium-4. Helium-4, with two protons and two neutrons, is the most tightly bound of the light nuclei. Most of the neutrons ended up there.

![The entire periodic table above helium was made later, in stars. These four light elements are what the first three minutes produced.](images/11-the-big-bang-fig-02.png)
*Figure 11.2 — Big Bang nucleosynthesis reaction network *

The arithmetic is straightforward. Start with the freeze-out ratio of one neutron per six protons. Take 2 neutrons and 12 protons. Form 1 helium-4 nucleus using both neutrons and 2 protons. You have 10 protons left over as hydrogen. The helium fraction by mass is $4/(4+10) = 4/14 \approx 28\%$. The actual calculation accounts for neutron decay (free neutrons are unstable, with a half-life of about ten minutes, so some neutrons decayed before they could be incorporated into helium) and gives a helium mass fraction of about 25%. Hydrogen makes up the remaining 75%.

Trace amounts of deuterium survive — about 1 part in 100,000 by number — because the reactions ran out of time before completing. The universe expanded and cooled too quickly for all the deuterium to be processed. Helium-3 and lithium-7 are produced in even smaller quantities.

By about 200 seconds — three minutes and twenty seconds — the temperature had fallen below 1 billion Kelvin, the nuclear reactions had effectively stopped, and the light element abundances were frozen. The universe was done with its first and only episode of primordial nucleosynthesis.

Now the comparison with observation. Measure the helium abundance in old, metal-poor stars — objects that formed early in the history of the galaxy, before much stellar nucleosynthesis had enriched the interstellar medium. Measure it in intergalactic gas clouds that have barely been processed. The measured helium fraction by mass is about 24 to 25%. That is exactly what Big Bang nucleosynthesis predicts.

Measure the deuterium abundance in ancient, barely-processed gas clouds along the line of sight to distant quasars. The measured value is about $2.5 \times 10^{-5}$ by number relative to hydrogen. That matches the prediction. And the deuterium abundance is extremely sensitive to the density of ordinary matter in the early universe — a small change in baryon density shifts the deuterium abundance substantially — so this measurement pins down the density of ordinary matter with precision.

There is one trouble: the observed lithium-7 abundance in the oldest stars is about a factor of three lower than predicted. This is called the lithium problem, and it remains unresolved. It may be that old stellar atmospheres have processed some lithium away over time. It may indicate an incomplete nuclear reaction network. It may require new physics. But helium and deuterium — produced in by far the largest quantities — agree perfectly. The framework is not overturned by the lithium discrepancy. It is constrained by it.

The deepest point about Big Bang nucleosynthesis is this: no other model of the universe predicts these abundances. If the universe had always been in its current state, or if it had started cool rather than hot, or if all elements were produced only in stars, the predicted ratios would be wrong. The agreement between calculation and observation works because there is a specific temperature-density history that threads through the calculation — and that history is the history of a hot Big Bang.

## The Light That Escaped When the Universe Turned Transparent

The third argument is the one Penzias and Wilson found.

For the first 380,000 years after the Big Bang, the universe was too hot for neutral atoms to exist. Anywhere in the universe, any hydrogen atom that managed to form would immediately be ionized by a photon — the electron stripped away. The universe was a plasma of free electrons and protons, and photons could not travel far without hitting a charged particle and scattering. The universe was opaque, not in the sense that there was no light but in the sense that light could not travel freely. Every photon kept bouncing off electrons and changing direction.

Then the universe cooled to about 3,000 Kelvin. At this temperature, electrons and protons could finally combine into neutral hydrogen atoms and stay combined. This is called recombination. In essentially a single cosmological moment, the free electrons vanished from the plasma, and the photons — which had been bouncing off electrons constantly — found nothing left to scatter from. They decoupled from matter and streamed freely outward.

Those photons have been traveling ever since. The universe has expanded by a factor of about 1,000 since recombination. The wavelengths of the photons have been stretched by the same factor — shifted from the near-infrared to microwaves. Their temperature has cooled from 3,000 Kelvin to about 2.73 Kelvin. They arrive from every direction because they decoupled everywhere simultaneously. The CMB is the last light from the era when the universe was opaque, and it fills the sky uniformly because the entire universe made it at the same moment.

Penzias and Wilson measured the temperature as 3.5 Kelvin, with their instrument uncertainties. The current best value from the Planck satellite is 2.72548 Kelvin. The prediction from the Big Bang model is about 2.73 Kelvin. The agreement is not a free parameter — the predicted temperature follows from the measured expansion rate and the physics of recombination.

![The entire visible color range spans four ten-thousandths of a degree. The early universe was uniform to one part in 100,000. The tiny variations you see here are the seeds of every galaxy that ever formed.](images/11-the-big-bang-fig-03.png)
*Figure 11.3 — Planck satellite full-sky CMB map *

The temperature is nearly uniform in all directions. Look in one direction and measure 2.72548 Kelvin. Look in the opposite direction and measure 2.72548 Kelvin. They agree to one part in 100,000. The early universe was extraordinarily uniform.

But not perfectly uniform. The tiny variations — a millionth of a degree warmer here, a millionth cooler there — carry a remarkable amount of information.

Before recombination, the plasma was not quite uniform. Regions of slightly higher density pulled in more matter through gravity, compressing and heating. The pressure pushed back, expanding and cooling the region. Gravity pulled it together again. The plasma oscillated — sound waves in the primordial gas, but at scales of hundreds of millions of light-years, propagating at about half the speed of light. When recombination happened and the photons decoupled, these oscillations were frozen in place. Their pattern imprinted itself on the temperature map of the CMB.

Today we can measure this pattern with extraordinary precision. The Planck satellite mapped the CMB temperature across the entire sky, and the fluctuation pattern contains sharp features — peaks in the power spectrum at specific angular scales. These peaks encode the composition and geometry of the universe in a way that can be calculated from first principles.

The first peak — corresponding to sound waves that had just completed one full oscillation by the time of recombination — encodes the geometry of space. If space is flat (which general relativity, given sufficient total energy density, predicts), the peak should appear at a specific angular scale. The measurement agrees with a flat universe to better than 0.1%. The universe is not a closed sphere or an open saddle. It is Euclidean.

The heights of the second and third peaks encode the relative densities of ordinary matter and dark matter. Dark matter does not interact with photons; ordinary matter does. This difference affects how the plasma oscillates. From the peak heights, one finds that ordinary matter makes up about 5% of the universe's total energy content. Dark matter — which does not interact with the plasma — makes up about 27%. Dark energy, inferred from the overall expansion history, makes up the remaining 68%.

![The agreement between theory and data is not approximate. The curve is calculated from first principles; the data points are measured. They match.](images/11-the-big-bang-fig-04.png)
*Figure 11.4 — CMB angular power spectrum *

These are not theoretical preferences. They are what the data require.

## Why Three Matters

Consider what it would take for this story to be wrong.

The expansion rate of the universe would have to be systematically miscalibrated. The physics of nuclear reactions at high temperatures — which we also test in laboratories and particle accelerators — would have to be wrong in just the right way. The CMB temperature, measured by multiple independent instruments over half a century, would have to be a systematic artifact. The light element abundances in the most primitive gas clouds in the universe would have to be contaminated in a way that mimics the predicted values. And all of these errors would have to conspire to give the same answer.

That is not impossible. But it is the alternative to believing the framework.

Notice what the three measurements are. The first — Hubble expansion — comes from the motions of galaxies measured by Doppler shifts in their optical spectra. The second — the CMB — comes from microwave temperature measurements of the sky. The third — light element abundances — comes from measuring isotope ratios in ancient gas clouds using ultraviolet and optical spectroscopy. These are three completely different physical phenomena, measured by completely different techniques, from completely different objects at completely different distances and times.

They all point to the same history.

The baryon density — the density of ordinary matter — is one place where two of the three measurements are directly comparable. Big Bang nucleosynthesis, through the deuterium abundance, pins down the baryon density. The CMB acoustic peaks, through the relative heights of the second and third oscillations, independently pin down the baryon density. The two values agree. They have to, if the story is right. They do agree. This is not a coincidence you can arrange by accident.

| Measurement Type | Physical Observable | Key Number Derived | Agrees with others? |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1) Hubble expansion | galaxy recession velocities | age ~13.8 Gyr, baryon density (indirect) | yes |
| 2) CMB acoustic peaks | temperature fluctuation pattern | baryon density ~4.9% of total energy, geometry flat to <0.1% | yes |
| 3) Big Bang nucleosynthesis | deuterium & helium abundances in ancient gas | baryon density ~4.9% of total energy, helium fraction ~25% | yes |

The universe began in a hot, dense state about 13.8 billion years ago. We know this from three independent observations. We can trace the physics from the first second onward with confidence. The hiss that Penzias and Wilson could not get rid of in 1964 was 13.8-billion-year-old light, cooling toward the ambient temperature of the universe, carrying in its nearly imperceptible temperature fluctuations the seeds of every galaxy and star that ever formed.

## What Is Not Yet Understood

The framework is solid. The questions at its edges are genuine.

The Hubble tension is real: different methods of measuring $H_0$ give slightly different values — about 67 km/s/Mpc from the CMB and about 73 km/s/Mpc from distance ladders built on supernovae. The discrepancy is now at about five standard deviations, which in physics is generally taken as evidence that something is wrong. Whether it is a systematic error in one of the measurements, or evidence of new physics in the early universe, is not yet resolved.

The physical nature of dark energy is unknown. We infer its existence from the accelerating expansion of the universe, and the simplest model — a cosmological constant, a fixed energy density of empty space — fits all the data. But quantum field theory predicts a vacuum energy density about $10^{120}$ times larger than what is observed, which is the most spectacular quantitative failure in the history of theoretical physics. Something about our understanding is deeply wrong.

The mechanism of inflation — the hypothesized period of rapid early expansion that made the universe so flat and so uniform — has theoretical motivations but no direct observational confirmation. The search for its predicted signature in the polarization of the CMB is ongoing.

And before $10^{-43}$ seconds, at the Planck time, our equations break down. Quantum gravity effects become important and we have no theory that handles them. Whatever happened at the very beginning — and whether "beginning" even makes sense as a concept — is beyond what we can currently calculate.

![Everything to the right of 380,000 years is directly observable. Everything between 1 second and 380,000 years is inferred from two observables that agree. Everything before 1 second is increasingly speculative.](images/11-the-big-bang-fig-05.png)
*Figure 11.5 — Cosmic timeline *

What is not at the frontier: the existence of the Big Bang itself, the age of the universe, the synthesis of the light elements, the origin of the CMB. These are as well-established as anything in science. The open questions are at the edges of what the theory explains. The core is solid.

Penzias and Wilson eventually learned what they had found, after a preprint was circulated from nearby Princeton, where a group had been building a telescope specifically to look for the radiation. They contacted the Princeton group, compared notes, and both published papers in the same issue of the Astrophysical Journal in 1965 — one announcing the discovery, the other explaining what it meant. Penzias and Wilson shared the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1978. The pigeon droppings, in the end, had nothing to do with it.
