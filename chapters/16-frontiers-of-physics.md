# Chapter 16 — Frontiers of Physics
*What two perfect theories, taken together, explain — and what they leave out.*

At 5:51 in the morning on September 14, 2015, two detectors — one in Hanford, Washington, and one in Livingston, Louisiana, separated by 3,000 kilometers — simultaneously recorded a signal. The signal was a chirp. It started low in frequency, swept upward over about 0.2 seconds, peaked near 250 Hz, and died away. The two recordings were nearly identical, arriving 7 milliseconds apart — the time it takes light to cross the distance between the two sites.

After three months of internal checking, LIGO announced the result on February 11, 2016. Two black holes, roughly 1.3 billion light-years away, one about 30 solar masses and one about 35, had spiraled together and merged into a single black hole of about 62 solar masses. Three solar masses had vanished — converted into gravitational-wave energy. The peak power output in the final milliseconds of the merger was roughly $3.6 \times 10^{49}$ watts — briefly exceeding the total electromagnetic luminosity of every star in the observable universe combined.

The result was a centennial vindication of a prediction Einstein had made in 1916. It confirmed that black holes exist as general relativity describes them. It opened a new observational window on the universe — gravitational-wave astronomy — that has since detected dozens more mergers, including, in 2017, two neutron stars colliding and producing gold, platinum, and other heavy elements in the process, answering a forty-year-old question in nuclear astrophysics.

This chapter is about the open frontier. Physics has explained an enormous amount. It has also left gaping holes — and intellectual honesty requires that we look at both together.

## The universe is expanding, and has been for 13.8 billion years

In 1929, Edwin Hubble published a paper based on measurements of 24 galaxies. He had recorded their distances (using Cepheid variable stars as distance markers) and their recession velocities (computed from the Doppler shift of their spectra). The result, plotted, was a roughly linear relationship: more distant galaxies are moving away from us faster. The equation is simple:

$$v = H_0 d,$$

where $H_0$ is now measured at about 70 km/s per megaparsec.

The implication is not that galaxies are flying outward through pre-existing space. Space itself is expanding, carrying the galaxies along. Run it backward and there must have been a moment when all the matter in the universe was concentrated to extreme density. That moment — the Big Bang — occurred about 13.8 billion years ago.

Two independent confirmations make this picture secure. In 1964, Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson discovered a faint microwave signal coming from every direction in the sky, at a temperature of 2.73 K. This is the cosmic microwave background (CMB) — the cooled remnant of radiation released about 380,000 years after the Big Bang, when the universe finally cooled enough for electrons to combine with protons into neutral hydrogen and stop scattering photons. The CMB has since been mapped to extraordinary precision. Its tiny temperature fluctuations — about $10^{-5}$ in relative amplitude — are the seeds from which galaxies later grew under gravity.

The second confirmation is the abundance of light elements. Big Bang nucleosynthesis — nuclear reactions in the first few minutes after the Big Bang, when temperatures were hot enough — predicts specific ratios of hydrogen, helium-4, helium-3, deuterium, and lithium-7. The observed abundances match the predictions.

A rough estimate of the universe's age follows directly. The inverse of Hubble's constant is:

$$t_H = \frac{1}{H_0} = \frac{1}{2.27 \times 10^{-18} \, \text{s}^{-1}} \approx 4.4 \times 10^{17} \, \text{s} \approx 14 \, \text{billion years.}$$

This is the "Hubble time" — how long galaxies would take to reach their current distances if they had always moved at their current velocities. The actual age (13.8 billion years, from detailed integration of the expansion history) is close, which is a check on the model's self-consistency.

The oldest stars in the Milky Way — in globular clusters — have ages of about 13 billion years. A star older than the universe would be a catastrophic failure of the model. So far: no such stars found.

![Hubble's 1929 data is the origin of modern cosmology — 24 galaxies, one of the most consequential plots in science.](images/16-frontiers-of-physics-fig-01.png)
*Figure 16.1 — Hubble diagram *

The contents of the universe, by mass-energy, are striking. About 5% is ordinary matter — atoms, ions, gas, everything that makes up all the stars and galaxies you can see. About 27% is dark matter. About 68% is dark energy. We are made of, and can observe, only the 5%.

## Gravity is the geometry of spacetime

In May 1919, Arthur Eddington photographed stars near the edge of the Sun's disk during a total solar eclipse, then compared the positions to the same stars photographed when the Sun was elsewhere. Starlight passing close to the Sun was bent by its gravity. Newtonian mechanics, treating light as having effective mass, predicts a deflection of 0.87 arcseconds for grazing rays. Einstein's general relativity, published in 1916, predicts 1.75 arcseconds. The two theories give different predictions, and the eclipse could distinguish them.

The measured deflection matched Einstein. The result was announced in November 1919, and Einstein became famous overnight.

General relativity treats gravity not as a force but as the curvature of spacetime. Mass and energy curve the spacetime around them; particles and light follow the straightest possible paths through that curved geometry. What we call gravitational attraction is the curvature making paths bend toward massive objects.

Einstein's starting point — the equivalence principle — was that there is no local experiment you can perform inside a freely falling sealed box that will tell you whether you are falling in a gravitational field or drifting in empty space with no gravity. The two situations are physically identical. From this principle Einstein argued that gravity must affect everything that acceleration affects — including light — and from this he derived the full field equations.

The predictions are precise and have all been confirmed:

Mercury's orbit precesses by 43 arcseconds per century more than Newtonian gravity predicts. Confirmed.

Light bends near massive objects — gravitational lensing — and this is now a routine tool in cosmology, used to map mass distributions in galaxy clusters. Confirmed.

Clocks run slower in stronger gravitational fields. GPS satellites orbit at 20,000 km altitude, where the Earth's gravity is weaker, so their clocks run slightly fast relative to clocks on the surface. The GPS system applies relativistic corrections; without them, position errors would accumulate at about 10 km per day. Confirmed routinely.

Black holes — regions of spacetime where the curvature is so extreme that nothing, not even light, can escape — are solutions to Einstein's equations. The event horizon of a non-rotating black hole of mass $M$ is the Schwarzschild radius:

$$r_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2}.$$

For one solar mass, this is about 3 km. For the Sun to become a black hole, you would need to compress it by a factor of $2 \times 10^5$ in linear dimension. The Sun is not going to do this. But when massive stars exhaust their nuclear fuel and collapse, the core does, and what is left is a neutron star (if the core mass is below about 3 solar masses) or a black hole (above that). These are not theoretical abstractions. They have been observed, their masses measured, their orbital dynamics studied. The LIGO mergers involved black holes of 30 to 60 solar masses behaving exactly as general relativity predicts.

Gravitational waves — ripples in the spacetime geometry itself, traveling at the speed of light — were also predicted in 1916 and are emitted by any accelerating mass distribution. The amplitude of the waves is tiny. The signal from GW150914, 1.3 billion light-years away, reached Earth as a strain of $10^{-21}$ — a 4-km interferometer arm changed length by $10^{-18}$ meters, less than a thousandth the diameter of a proton. LIGO detected this directly with laser interferometry. The precision required is extraordinary: it is as if you were measuring the distance from here to the nearest star, Alpha Centauri, to within the width of a human hair.

![The strain of 10⁻²¹ means a 4-km arm changed by 10⁻¹⁸ m — less than a proton's diameter. The template match is exact.](images/16-frontiers-of-physics-fig-03.png)
*Figure 16.3 — LIGO gravitational-wave detection *

The incompleteness of general relativity is well-defined. GR is a classical theory. It says nothing about quantum mechanics. At the center of a black hole, and in the first $10^{-43}$ seconds of the Big Bang, the spacetime curvature becomes so extreme that quantum-gravitational effects must dominate — and we have no theory for this regime. Every candidate theory (string theory, loop quantum gravity, others) has produced beautiful mathematics and no confirmed predictions that differ from the classical theory. The problem is not that we lack ideas; it is that the energy scale at which quantum gravity matters ($\sim 10^{19}$ GeV, the Planck scale) is about fifteen orders of magnitude beyond what any conceivable particle accelerator will ever reach. Testing quantum gravity may require cosmological observations rather than laboratory experiments.

## Most of the universe is missing

Vera Rubin spent years in the 1970s measuring how fast stars orbit the centers of spiral galaxies — the galactic rotation curves. From Newton's law of gravity, the orbital speed of a star at distance $r$ from the galactic center should be determined by the mass enclosed within $r$. Far outside the visible distribution of stars and gas, where little mass is left to enclose, the speed should fall off as $1/\sqrt{r}$ — just as outer planets orbit the Sun more slowly than inner ones.

The speed did not fall off. It stayed flat.

![The flat part is the problem. There is no visible mass out there to support those speeds.](images/16-frontiers-of-physics-fig-04.png)
*Figure 16.4 — Galactic rotation curve *

To support a flat rotation curve at $v = 200$ km/s out to 30 kiloparsecs, the enclosed mass must be:

$$M(r) = \frac{v^2 r}{G} = \frac{(2 \times 10^5)^2 \times (9.26 \times 10^{20})}{6.67 \times 10^{-11}} \approx 5.6 \times 10^{41} \, \text{kg} \approx 2.8 \times 10^{11} \, M_\odot.$$

The visible stars and gas in the galaxy account for perhaps $5 \times 10^{10}$ solar masses. The mass required is five times more. The extra mass is distributed in an extended halo around the galaxy — unseen, because it does not emit, absorb, or scatter light.

This is dark matter. Fritz Zwicky had noticed something similar in clusters of galaxies in 1933 — the galaxies in a cluster move too fast to be held by the visible matter alone — but his result was ignored for decades. Rubin's rotation curves, repeated for galaxy after galaxy, were impossible to dismiss. Independent confirmations piled up: gravitational lensing of distant galaxies by foreground clusters produces distortions too large to be explained by visible mass alone. The pattern of acoustic oscillations in the CMB requires dark matter to be present. Galaxy formation — the growth of structure from tiny density fluctuations — would not have happened in the time available without dark matter providing the gravitational seed.

The fraction is not small. About 85% of all matter in the universe is dark matter. We know it gravitates. We know it doesn't interact electromagnetically. We don't know what it is.

Direct detection experiments — sensitive detectors deep underground, looking for the recoil of an atomic nucleus hit by a passing dark-matter particle — have been running for decades. The most sensitive of them (LUX-ZEPLIN, XENONnT) have found nothing. Many candidate models — weakly interacting massive particles (WIMPs) in various mass ranges — have been ruled out. The space of possible dark-matter candidates is being narrowed, but the answer is not yet in hand. Anyone who tells you dark matter will be discovered in the next five years is expressing hope, not knowledge.

Dark energy is a different problem with a different character. In 1998, two teams studying Type Ia supernovae at high redshift — the Supernova Cosmology Project (Saul Perlmutter) and the High-Z Supernova Team (Brian Schmidt and Adam Riess) — found that distant supernovae were dimmer than expected. They were farther away than they should have been in a decelerating universe. The universe's expansion is not slowing down. It is accelerating.

To accelerate the expansion of the universe, something must be acting against gravity on cosmic scales — a component with negative pressure, uniformly distributed through all of space. This is dark energy. The CMB power spectrum and large-scale structure independently confirm its presence. The Nobel Prize went to Perlmutter, Schmidt, and Riess in 2011.

The simplest model — a cosmological constant $\Lambda$ in Einstein's equations, representing a fixed energy density of the vacuum — fits all the data. Einstein himself introduced $\Lambda$ in 1917 (for unrelated reasons), then called it his "greatest blunder" when Hubble's expansion was discovered. Ironically, the vacuum energy is real after all, just not for the reason Einstein originally put it there.

The problem is that nobody understands why $\Lambda$ has the value it has. Quantum field theory predicts a vacuum energy density. It predicts it to about $10^{120}$ times too large. The ratio of the predicted value to the observed value is $10^{120}$ — a number so large that the mismatch has no precedent in physics. Either the quantum-field-theory prediction is wrong in a way we don't understand, or there is some cancellation mechanism of extraordinary precision. This is called the cosmological constant problem and is, by any measure of quantitative discrepancy, the worst failure of a successful theory in the history of physics.

## The honest map

Let me say plainly what we know and what we don't.

We have two extraordinarily successful theories. The Standard Model describes all matter and all non-gravitational forces with precision that has never been contradicted by any experiment — checked at energy scales from meV to TeV, across processes from atomic spectroscopy to collider physics. General relativity describes gravity and spacetime from the solar system to the scale of the observable universe, with every quantitative prediction confirmed.

Together, they account for everything we have ever observed in a laboratory or a detector.

Together, they account for about 5% of the universe's total mass-energy content.

The catalog of known incompleteness is long. Dark matter: something accounts for 85% of all matter, and we don't know what it is. Dark energy: something accounts for 68% of the universe's energy density, and we don't know what it is, and the naive theoretical estimate for it is $10^{120}$ times too large. Quantum gravity: there is no theory of gravity that is consistent with quantum mechanics; neither GR nor quantum field theory describes what happens at the center of a black hole or in the first $10^{-43}$ seconds of the universe. Matter-antimatter asymmetry: the universe contains matter and almost no antimatter, but the Standard Model predicts they should have been produced in nearly equal amounts; something is wrong somewhere. Neutrino masses: neutrinos oscillate between types, which requires mass, but the Standard Model predicted them to be massless; the mechanism is unclear. The hierarchy problem: why is the Higgs mass 125 GeV rather than $10^{19}$ GeV? Why three generations of fermions rather than one, or seven? Why these 19 particular parameter values in the Standard Model?

None of these is a minor puzzle. Each is a place where the current edifice has a known crack.

I want to say something about the nature of this situation, because it is easy to read the list above and conclude either that physics is in crisis or that physics has failed. Neither is right. Physics is in the position of a theory that has passed every test it has been given, while knowing that it cannot be the final theory because it leaves so much unexplained. This is not a failure. This is how progress works. Newtonian mechanics was extraordinarily successful for two centuries before it turned out to be wrong in the regime of very high speeds and very strong gravity. It wasn't wrong in the domain where it was tested; it was incomplete. The Standard Model and GR are the same: right where tested, incomplete in ways we can identify precisely.

The remarkable thing is that physics has reached the point where the incompleteness is well-characterized. We know what we don't know. The specific questions — what is dark matter, why is $\Lambda$ so small, how does gravity quantize — are sharper than they have ever been. That is progress, even if the answers haven't come yet.

The detection of gravitational waves in 2015 is a good example of what this frontier looks like in practice. A prediction made a century ago, requiring an engineering achievement that most physicists thought impossible (measuring $10^{-18}$ m displacements in 4-km arms over human-scale timescales), confirmed in a single chirp of data. The LIGO result did not solve any of the open problems listed above. But it opened a new observational channel. Gravitational-wave observations will eventually map the neutron-star equation of state, constrain models of black hole formation, and possibly detect a background of gravitational waves from the early universe — which might, finally, give us direct observational access to physics at energy scales where the Standard Model and GR both break down.

That's the frontier: precise questions, incomplete theories, new instruments, and the willingness to say "we don't know yet" without pretending otherwise.

Physics has explained an enormous amount and is far from done. Both clauses are equally important, and both are equally true.

### Warm-up

**16.1** *(Big Bang evidence)* State three independent pieces of observational evidence for the Big Bang model. For each, name the specific observation and what it confirms.

**16.2** *(equivalence principle)* State the equivalence principle in one sentence. Give one example of a prediction it implies — other than the bending of light.

**16.3** *(dark matter vs. dark energy)* In two sentences each, distinguish dark matter from dark energy. What does each one do? What is each one's evidence?

**16.4** *(Schwarzschild radius)* The formula $r_s = 2GM/c^2$ gives the event-horizon radius of a non-rotating black hole. Without calculating, predict whether $r_s$ increases or decreases as the black hole mass increases. Then confirm with the formula.

### Application

**16.5** *(Hubble time)* Using $H_0 = 70$ km/s/Mpc, convert $H_0$ to SI units (s$^{-1}$) and compute the Hubble time $t_H = 1/H_0$ in seconds and in gigayears. Compare to the independently determined age of the universe (13.8 Gyr) and explain why they are not identical.

**16.6** *(Schwarzschild radii)* Compute the Schwarzschild radius for (a) a 10-solar-mass stellar black hole, (b) a $10^8$-solar-mass supermassive black hole, (c) a hypothetical black hole of Earth's mass ($M_\oplus = 6.0 \times 10^{24}$ kg). Express each in meters and in a physically intuitive unit (km, AU, etc.).

**16.7** *(dark matter rotation curve)* A galaxy has a flat rotation curve at $v = 250$ km/s out to $r = 50$ kpc ($1 \text{ kpc} = 3.086 \times 10^{19}$ m). (a) Compute the enclosed mass. (b) If the visible mass is $8 \times 10^{10} \, M_\odot$, what fraction of the enclosed mass is dark? (c) Is this consistent with the cosmological dark-to-visible ratio of roughly 5:1?

**16.8** *(LIGO strain)* GW150914 produced a strain of $h = 10^{-21}$ at LIGO's 4-km arms. (a) Compute the change in arm length $\Delta L = h \times L$. (b) Express this as a fraction of: a proton diameter (~$10^{-15}$ m), a hydrogen atom diameter (~$10^{-10}$ m), a human hair (~$70 \, \mu$m). Why is this detection considered a landmark engineering achievement?

### Synthesis

**16.9** *(three-evidence convergence on dark matter)* Galactic rotation curves, gravitational lensing by galaxy clusters, and CMB acoustic oscillations all independently point to the same dark-matter-to-baryonic-matter ratio (~5:1). Explain, in your own words and in a few sentences for each, the physical reasoning that connects each observation to that conclusion. Why does the same ratio emerging from three entirely different physical phenomena make the dark-matter case so strong?

**16.10** *(cosmological constant problem)* The observed dark-energy density is approximately $\rho_\Lambda \approx 6 \times 10^{-27}$ kg/m$^3$. Quantum field theory naively predicts a vacuum energy density $\rho_{\text{QFT}} \approx 10^{93}$ kg/m$^3$. (a) Compute the ratio $\rho_{\text{QFT}} / \rho_\Lambda$. (b) This is often quoted as $10^{120}$ — reconcile this with your answer (hint: the discrepancy depends on how the comparison is set up). (c) In one sentence, explain why this is considered the worst quantitative discrepancy between a successful theory and observation in the history of physics.

**16.11** *(the 5% universe)* The Standard Model successfully describes 5% of the universe's mass-energy. List four open problems that, if solved, would significantly enlarge that fraction. For each, state (a) the observation that reveals the gap, and (b) what would count as a solution.

### Challenge

**16.12** *(GW energy)* The merger GW150914 converted approximately 3 solar masses to gravitational-wave energy. (a) Compute this energy in joules using $E = mc^2$. (b) The event lasted about 0.2 seconds. Estimate the average power output. (c) The total luminosity of all stars in the observable universe is roughly $10^{49}$ W. How does the peak gravitational-wave power compare? (d) Why doesn't this enormous power output have any observable effect on Earth, beyond the $10^{-21}$ strain LIGO detected?

**16.13** *(Hubble tension)* The Hubble constant measured from the CMB (Planck satellite, early-universe method) gives $H_0 \approx 67.4$ km/s/Mpc. Measurements from Cepheid-calibrated Type Ia supernovae (late-universe method) give $H_0 \approx 73$ km/s/Mpc. The disagreement is about 5σ. (a) Compute the Hubble time for each value. How many gigayears apart are the implied ages? (b) Why is a 5σ discrepancy between two well-tested methods considered a potential signal of new physics rather than a measurement error? (c) Name one possible physical explanation if the tension is real (new dark-energy behavior, additional relativistic species, modified gravity, etc.).
